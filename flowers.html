Cherry Blossom

Cherry blossoms (Prunus serrulata) are the iconic flowers of spring in Japan, where the annual bloom is celebrated during hanami festivals. The delicate pink and white petals last only one to two weeks, making them a symbol of the fleeting beauty of life.

Cherry trees thrive in temperate climates and need a period of winter chill to bloom properly. While Japan is most famous for its cherry blossoms, they also flourish in Washington D.C., South Korea, and parts of Europe.

Did you know? Japan has over 200 varieties of cherry blossom trees, and the Japanese Meteorological Agency tracks the "cherry blossom front" as it moves northward each spring.

Daisy

The common daisy (Bellis perennis) is one of the most recognizable wildflowers in the world. Its name comes from "day's eye" because the flower opens at dawn and closes at dusk. What appears to be a single flower is actually a composite of dozens of tiny florets arranged in a disc.

Daisies belong to the Asteraceae family, making them relatives of sunflowers and chrysanthemums. They grow on every continent except Antarctica and have been used in folk medicine for centuries to treat wounds and inflammation.

Did you know? A single daisy "flower" is actually made up of two types of florets: white ray florets around the edge and yellow disc florets in the center.

Rose

Roses (Rosa) have been cultivated for over 5,000 years and are the world's most popular ornamental flower. There are over 30,000 registered varieties ranging from miniature groundcovers to climbing plants that can reach 7 meters tall. The classic red rose has become a universal symbol of love and passion.

Beyond their beauty, roses are surprisingly practical. Rose hips are rich in vitamin C and used in teas and jams. Rose water and rose oil (attar of roses) are used extensively in perfumery, cosmetics, and Middle Eastern cuisine. It takes roughly 10,000 pounds of rose petals to produce a single pound of rose oil.

Did you know? The world's oldest living rose bush grows on the wall of Hildesheim Cathedral in Germany and is believed to be over 1,000 years old.

Sunflower

The sunflower (Helianthus annuus) is native to the Americas and was domesticated around 3000 BCE by Native Americans. Young sunflowers exhibit heliotropism, tracking the sun from east to west during the day, though mature flowers typically face east permanently to warm up faster and attract more pollinators.

A single sunflower head can contain up to 2,000 seeds arranged in interlocking spirals that follow the Fibonacci sequence. Sunflowers are one of the most economically important flowers in the world, cultivated for their oil, seeds, and as animal feed. Ukraine and Russia together produce over half the world's sunflower oil.

Did you know? Sunflowers can be used for phytoremediation, absorbing toxic heavy metals and radioactive contaminants from soil. They were planted around Chernobyl and Fukushima after nuclear disasters.

Tulip

Tulips (Tulipa) originated in Central Asia and were first cultivated by the Ottoman Empire before being introduced to Europe in the 16th century. The Netherlands became so obsessed with tulips that during the 1630s, Tulip Mania drove prices to extraordinary levels — a single bulb of the prized "Semper Augustus" variety reportedly sold for more than a canal house in Amsterdam.

Today the Netherlands produces roughly 3 billion tulip bulbs annually, covering about 11,000 hectares. Tulips come in nearly every color except true blue, and they continue to grow after being cut — a vase of tulips can gain 2–3 cm in height over a few days.

Did you know? During the Dutch famine of 1944–45, tulip bulbs were eaten as a last resort. They were sliced, dried, and ground into flour for baking.
